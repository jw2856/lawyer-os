# Antitrust & Competition Law

## Key US Statutes
- **Sherman Act**: Prohibits agreements in restraint of trade (Section 1) and monopolization (Section 2)
- **Clayton Act**: Prohibits mergers/acquisitions that substantially lessen competition; price discrimination
- **FTC Act**: Prohibits unfair methods of competition and unfair/deceptive acts
- **Hart-Scott-Rodino**: Pre-merger notification filing requirements above thresholds

## Key EU Law
- **TFEU Article 101**: Prohibits anti-competitive agreements (cartels, price-fixing, market allocation)
- **TFEU Article 102**: Prohibits abuse of dominant position
- **EU Merger Regulation**: Pre-merger notification for transactions meeting thresholds
- **National competition authorities**: Each member state has enforcement authority

## Common Issues in Commercial Agreements
- **Most-favored-nation (MFN) clauses**: Can raise antitrust concerns — evaluate whether they dampen competition
- **Exclusivity arrangements**: Exclusive dealing can be anticompetitive if market power exists
- **Non-compete clauses**: Between competitors = per se illegal; ancillary to legitimate transaction = may be permissible
- **Information sharing**: Sharing competitively sensitive information (pricing, costs, customers) with competitors is high risk
- **Resale price maintenance**: Setting minimum resale prices can violate antitrust law
- **Tying and bundling**: Conditioning purchase of one product on another

## Merger Control
- **HSR filing thresholds**: Size of transaction and size of parties tests (adjusted annually)
- **Waiting period**: 30 days (can be extended with second request)
- **International filings**: Many jurisdictions have their own merger control regimes
- **Gun-jumping**: Integrating before clearance is illegal

## Risk Mitigation
- Train employees on antitrust compliance
- Review partnership and joint venture agreements for competitive concerns
- Be cautious with competitor communications (trade associations, conferences)
- Document legitimate business justifications for potentially restrictive terms
