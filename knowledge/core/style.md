## Structure Patterns

### Memos and Analysis
1. **Executive summary first** -- The reader should get the bottom line without reading further.
2. **Background** -- Only what's needed to understand the analysis.
3. **Analysis** -- Dense, substantive sections. Each covers one issue thoroughly.
4. **Options** -- Alternatives in parallel structure with trade-offs.
5. **Key takeaway** -- Restate core conclusion and decision point.

### Options Presentation
Always present options with:
- Clear label
- What it involves (1-2 sentences)
- Sub-options if applicable
- Residual risk after implementing
- Operational/business implications
- Effort estimate when possible

## Language Preferences

### Do
- Use active voice
- Quantify risk (high/significant/moderate/low)
- Use "we" and "our" for internal documents
- Use precise legal terms but explain when audience may not know them
- Connect every risk to a business consequence

### Don't
- Hedge unnecessarily
- Use passive voice when active is clearer
- Present problems without options or next steps
- Use filler phrases
- Over-qualify with stacked caveats

## Formatting

- **Executive summary**: Bullet points for key findings
- **Analysis sections**: Full paragraphs -- don't bullet-point through legal analysis
- **Options**: Structured with labels, sub-options, consistent format
- **Recommendations/next steps**: Bullet points, action-oriented
- **Headers**: Clear, descriptive, used liberally for organization

## Risk Language Calibration

| Term | When to use |
|------|------------|
| "High risk" | Likely to result in adverse outcome if not addressed |
| "Significant risk" | Material probability; serious consequences |
| "Moderate risk" | Present but manageable with reasonable controls |
| "Low risk" | Unlikely but possible; acknowledge and move on |
| "Residual risk" | Risk remaining after mitigation measures |
